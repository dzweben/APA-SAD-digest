The Social Brain Dispatch

The Blueprint and the Patient

Michael Davis mapped the neural architecture of fear in rats for two decades; the first fMRI study of social anxiety confirmed what that map predicted and exposed the question it was never designed to answer.

Edition No. 3 · Foundational and Landmark Neuroscience · June 29, 2026

The neural architecture of social anxiety disorder did not emerge from the study of social anxiety. It came from rats, acoustic startle, and a laboratory at Yale’s Connecticut Mental Health Center that had been systematically dismantling conditioned fear circuitry since the early 1970s. By the time the first functional magnetic resonance imaging study of social phobia appeared in the spring of 1998, the anxiety field had inherited a model of fear that was built for a different purpose entirely. The question of whether that model fit the human disorder was answered quickly, and affirmatively. The question of how well it fit, and what it missed, has occupied the field for nearly thirty years without a complete resolution.

To understand what was at stake requires starting not with patients but with animals and startle reflexes.

Michael Davis had been studying acoustic startle since the late 1960s. He eventually joined the faculty at Yale’s Ribicoff Research Facilities, housed within the Connecticut Mental Health Center, where he would spend more than two decades before moving to Emory University as the Robert W. Woodruff Professor of Psychiatry and Behavioral Sciences. Davis operated from a methodological conviction stated plainly in his writing: fear and anxiety are private internal states accessible to experimental science only through their behavioral and physiological readouts, and the most informative readout is one that is simultaneously graded, objective, and neuroanatomically interpretable. The acoustic startle reflex satisfied all three conditions. The whole-body flinch produced by a sudden loud noise is a reflexive, species-wide response whose brainstem circuitry, from the cochlear nucleus through the nucleus reticularis pontis caudalis and out to spinal motor neurons, was anatomically well-characterized before Davis arrived on it. It expressed continuously as a graded amplitude measurement, responded to pharmacological and surgical interventions in interpretable ways, and, crucially, could be modulated by emotional state.

The paradigm Davis built on this foundation is fear-potentiated startle. A rat is trained to associate a neutral conditioned stimulus, most often a light, with a brief foot shock. The pairing is sufficient to make the light a signal of impending harm. When an acoustic click is subsequently delivered in the presence of the conditioned light, the startle response is substantially larger than the same click delivered in darkness. That amplification, which in well-trained animals can reach fifty to one hundred percent above baseline startle amplitude, is the measure of conditioned fear. The click is physically identical in both conditions; what differs is the state the conditioned stimulus induces, and that state amplifies the basic reflex. Because the fear state is expressed through a reflex whose neural substrate was already anatomically known, any manipulation that affected the amplification could be located within a specific circuit, making the paradigm not merely a behavioral assay but a neuroanatomical dissection tool.

The 1992 Annual Review of Neuroscience paper is the consolidated account of what two decades of this approach had revealed. Davis had spent the 1980s systematically lesioning, stimulating, and pharmacologically probing amygdaloid structures while measuring the effects on fear-potentiated startle and related conditioned fear indices. By 1992 the picture was clear enough for a comprehensive synthesis. The paper’s central argument is that the central nucleus of the amygdala functions as the obligatory output node for conditioned fear, and that its efferent projections to multiple brainstem and hypothalamic targets generate the full phenomenology of fear as a coordinated, parallel set of response streams rather than as a unitary state triggered by a single switch.

The evidence for the central nucleus’ necessity was converging from multiple angles. Bilateral lesions of the structure abolished fear-potentiated startle without affecting baseline startle amplitude, establishing that it was required for the conditioned amplification but not for the underlying reflex. Electrical stimulation of the central nucleus produced behavioral and autonomic changes characteristic of fear without any conditioned stimulus being present. Chemical inactivation and localized drug infusions showed that the central nucleus was not merely a relay but an active site where the learned fear association was represented and from which the output was distributed. The input side of the model placed the basolateral amygdaloid complex upstream of the central nucleus, receiving projections from sensory thalamus via a fast, coarse-grained route and from sensory cortex via a slower, more refined one, and passing the threat signal forward.

Davis mapped the central nucleus’s efferent targets with a specificity that remains foundational. Projections to the lateral hypothalamus engage the sympathetic nervous system and produce the autonomic signature of fear: elevated heart rate and blood pressure, pupil dilation, and piloerection. Projections to the dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus regulate parasympathetic cardiac function. Projections to the nucleus reticularis pontis caudalis, the same brainstem nucleus whose output Davis was recording as his startle amplitude readout, explained mechanistically why central nucleus activation amplifies the startle reflex. Projections to the paraventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus drive corticotropin-releasing factor and ACTH release, initiating the HPA axis stress response that sustains elevated cortisol. Projections to the periaqueductal gray produce behavioral freezing and stress-induced analgesia. Projections to the locus coeruleus trigger norepinephrine-mediated arousal and attentional mobilization. Projections to the basal forebrain enhance cholinergic vigilance and sensory gating.

The architecture is not a single switch but a broadcast system. When the central nucleus fires, it simultaneously drives each of these targets, generating the whole package that we recognize as fear: the animal freezes, its heart races, its stress hormones surge, its startle amplitude spikes, its attention narrows onto the source of threat. Each component is separately dissociable by targeted interventions, which was the key empirical confirmation of the model’s structure. Disrupting specific output projections selectively abolished the corresponding behavioral or physiological component while leaving others intact. This modularity was a conceptual advance: it replaced an undifferentiated concept of fear with a mechanistically specified account of how a hub structure generates a multi-component response through parallel effector lines, each of which could in principle be targeted independently.

Corticotropin-releasing factor occupied a prominent place in Davis’s account. Infusing CRF into the amygdala or the ventricular system increased baseline startle amplitude and potentiated conditioned fear responses; CRF antagonists reduced them. The peptide appeared to function as a gain modulator for the fear output network, raising the sensitivity of the entire system and contributing to the transition from acute defensive response to sustained anxious arousal. Davis discussed at length how tonic or chronically elevated CRF-mediated amygdala output might underlie the persistent anxiety states seen in clinical populations. He was appropriately careful about the distance between rat pharmacology and human psychopathology, but the implication was clear: the same circuit that generates acute conditioned fear in rodents could, under conditions of sustained CRF elevation, generate the chronic apprehension that defines anxiety disorders.

The 1992 paper gave the anxiety field something it had not previously had: a mechanistically specified fear circuit established from multiple independent experimental methods, with testable predictions at every level from pharmacology to behavior. When amygdala activation began appearing in PET and fMRI studies of emotion in the mid-1990s, it carried real interpretive weight because Davis had already established, from animal work, that the amygdala was not merely correlated with fear but necessary for it. The field had a mechanism, not just a correlation. What it did not yet have was evidence that the mechanism was the right one for human social fear specifically.

In April 1998, a paper appeared in NeuroReport carrying the direct title “fMRI reveals amygdala activation to human faces in social phobics.” The first author was Niels Birbaumer of the Institute of Medical Psychology and Behavioral Neurobiology at the University of Tübingen, working alongside Wolfgang Grodd, Tübingen’s principal neuroimaging researcher, and Herta Flor, whose primary research program centered on pain and aversive conditioning. Several junior colleagues across radiology and behavioral neuroscience completed the team. Their combined profile was unusual for the period: not a dedicated anxiety clinic doing neuroimaging, and not a cognitive neuroscience group that had turned toward clinical populations, but an interdisciplinary group whose expertise straddled basic conditioning science, clinical psychophysiology, and the emerging technology of functional brain imaging.

Birbaumer himself was, in 1998, not primarily known for social anxiety research. He was a Viennese psychophysiologist whose career had been built around the intersection of basic brain dynamics and clinical application, most prominently through his program developing brain-computer interface systems for patients with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis who had lost all voluntary motor function. Beginning in the early 1990s, his group trained severely paralyzed patients to regulate the slow cortical potentials in their EEG, shifting the low-frequency oscillations that reflect cortical excitability up or down, and used those self-regulated signals as a binary switch to operate a computer cursor. The work required sustained expertise in recording and modulating human brain signals under precisely controlled conditions, and gave Birbaumer both the technical infrastructure for brain measurement in clinical populations and the intellectual habit of asking neurophysiological questions about disorders that others were approaching through behavior and self-report alone. What he brought to the social phobia study was the willingness to put a clinical population in a scanner and look for a signal the existing literature had established reason to expect but had not yet found.

It is worth noting, as part of the honest intellectual history of this field, that Birbaumer’s later career became deeply contested. In the late 2010s, his research claiming that patients in a completely locked-in state could use a BCI device to signal subjective well-being was scrutinized, found to contain unreliable data, and retracted by PLOS Biology. Subsequent investigations by the German Research Foundation found the research record incomplete and the reported results inadequately supported, leading to loss of major funding and his removal from later academic positions. None of this touched the 1998 social phobia paper, which addressed a different population, used different methods, and asked different questions, and which has never been questioned; its core finding has been independently replicated many times. But the arc of a scientist’s career is part of the intellectual record. The first neuroimaging study of social anxiety disorder was authored by someone who became, in a different domain and nearly two decades later, one of the more conspicuous cases of scientific misconduct in recent German neuroscience.

The study enrolled seven adults meeting then-current diagnostic criteria for social phobia and five healthy controls without psychiatric history. Scanning used a 1.5-tesla Siemens system, with acquisition restricted to a limited number of coronal slices centered on the amygdaloid region rather than covering the whole brain. This was a characteristic constraint of early fMRI at that field strength: signal-to-noise in temporolimbic structures was modest, whole-brain coverage was slow, and a focused acquisition was a reasonable compromise for a hypothesis-driven study targeting a specific structure.

The paradigm presented two categories of stimuli in alternating blocks: photographs of neutral human faces, and aversive olfactory stimuli. The experimental logic was precise. Social phobia is defined by fear of social evaluation, and faces are the primary social stimulus available in a laboratory context. Aversive odors are unpleasant but carry no social signal; they tax general aversive processing without the social valence that defines the phobic stimulus class. If the amygdala hyperreactivity in social phobia reflects a generic elevation in aversive sensitivity, both stimulus types should produce elevated amygdala response in patients relative to controls. If the hyperreactivity is specific to social input, faces should drive the group difference while odors should not. The design operationalized the specificity hypothesis as directly as any paradigm of the era could manage.

The results were unambiguous within the sample’s constraints. Bilateral amygdala activation was present in the social phobia group during the face condition and absent in the healthy controls viewing the same photographs. Aversive odors produced bilateral amygdala activation in both groups, with no significant between-group difference. Social phobics were not characterized by a globally lowered threshold for aversive processing. What set them apart was the amygdaloid response to social visual stimuli specifically. The structure Davis had established as the hub of conditioned fear was selectively hyperreactive, in a human clinical population, to the stimulus class that defines social threat for that population.

The sample provides an existence proof, not a parameter estimate. Seven patients cannot support precise inference about effect size, laterality, or which amygdaloid subdivision was involved. The restricted slice coverage made prefrontal connectivity and downstream regulatory circuits inaccessible. An existence proof can nonetheless shift a field, and this one did. Within the following decade, replications extended and refined the signal. Stein and colleagues (2002) demonstrated hyperreactivity to angry and contemptuous faces in an event-related paradigm with a substantially larger sample. Subsequent work showed that the amygdala response persisted even when emotional faces were presented so briefly, and then immediately masked by a neutral face, that subjects reported no conscious awareness of having seen them, suggesting that the hyperreactive response operates through rapid subcortical routes preceding full cortical face recognition. Each replication confirmed the existence proof and added resolution to a picture the Birbaumer paper had sketched at the lowest available grain.

The convergence between Davis (1992) and Birbaumer and colleagues (1998) is historically consequential and genuinely earned. Davis established from animal work that the central nucleus of the amygdala is necessary for conditioned fear expression and that its output pathways generate the full fear syndrome through parallel effector streams. Birbaumer and colleagues established that the amygdala is hyperreactive to social stimuli in human social phobia, and that the hyperreactivity is specific to social input rather than reflecting generic aversive sensitivity. The inferential chain from animal model to clinical relevance is closed: a structure proved necessary for conditioned fear in animals is over-engaged in a human fear disorder, and the over-engagement is specific to the stimulus class that defines the disorder. Convergences of this kind, across species and across methodological traditions, are exactly what a translational program needs to feel confident it is measuring something real rather than a confound particular to one paradigm or one population.

The convergence also provided a productive therapeutic logic. If Davis’s conditioned fear circuit applies to social anxiety, then interventions that disrupt conditioned associations within the basolateral-central amygdala axis should attenuate the disorder. Exposure therapy, the empirical core of psychological treatment for SAD, works mechanistically along this logic: repeated non-reinforced presentation of feared social stimuli allows fear-conditioned associations to extinguish, vmPFC-mediated inhibitory circuits to suppress the conditioned amygdala response, and safety memories to consolidate across successful social encounters. That exposure therapy is effective for SAD, though not universally so and with durability that varies considerably, is at least consistent with the conditioned fear account that the Davis-Birbaumer convergence implied.

The tensions between the papers are equally real, and neither the replication literature nor subsequent theoretical work has resolved them. The first concerns what the activating stimulus actually means. Davis’s paradigm uses arbitrary conditioned stimuli: lights and tones that have no intrinsic relationship to the aversive unconditioned event and that acquire their fear-signaling function entirely through associative experience. The neutral faces in Birbaumer’s paradigm are not arbitrary in this sense. Faces are among the most computationally privileged categories of visual input in the primate nervous system. They are processed through a dedicated subcortical pathway running from the superior colliculus through the pulvinar to the amygdala, a route that operates before full cortical face recognition is complete and that is preferentially sensitive to biologically relevant social cues including gaze direction and emotional expression. Human neonates preferentially orient to face-like configurations within hours of birth, before any associative learning with aversive outcomes could have been established. When social phobics show amygdala hyperreactivity to neutral faces, they may be showing conditioned fear in Davis’s sense: prior experiences of social rejection or public embarrassment have associated face stimuli with aversive outcomes, and the amygdala fires to what has become a conditioned social cue. Or they may be showing something structurally different: a dispositional hypersensitivity of the face-to-amygdala input pathway, a system set at baseline to treat social signals as potentially threatening in a way that requires no specific conditioning history.

This distinction matters clinically and is not resolvable by a cross-sectional study conducted once in patients who already carry the disorder. If social anxiety reflects conditioned associations, extinction procedures should in principle dissolve them, and the mechanism is the same as for any acquired fear. If social anxiety reflects constitutive input-pathway hypersensitivity, the relevant target may instead be the gain of the upstream social detection system, a question about architecture rather than learning history, and one for which standard extinction procedures would be insufficient or would need to be supplemented by something different. What the neutral-face paradigm established is that the hyperreactive amygdala response extends to social stimuli carrying no explicit evaluative content, not only to angry or contemptuous expressions. That is an important constraint on the simple conditioning hypothesis, though not a refutation.

The second tension is more fundamental, and it emerges from within Davis’s own program. In the years after the 1992 Annual Review paper, Davis’s group continued the circuit mapping and made a discovery that reorganized the taxonomy of fear and anxiety. Working with David Walker and colleagues, Davis established that the central nucleus of the amygdala and the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis, structures anatomically continuous but functionally distinct, dissociate the two temporal profiles of defensive responding. Lesions of the central nucleus specifically blocked fear-potentiated startle to brief, discrete conditioned stimuli but left largely intact the sustained startle potentiation produced by unpredictable threat or by an anxiogenic context. Lesions of the bed nucleus produced the reverse: sustained, context-dependent apprehension was attenuated while phasic conditioned fear responses remained. Walker and Davis (1997) published this double dissociation in the Journal of Neuroscience. Davis, Walker, Miles, and Grillon (2010) subsequently elaborated the framework into a comprehensive account of phasic fear, mediated by the central amygdala and driven by discrete conditioned stimuli, versus sustained anxiety, mediated by the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis and driven by unpredictable or contextual threat in conditions of temporal uncertainty. Corticotropin-releasing factor, it turned out, was far more densely expressed in the bed nucleus than in the central amygdala itself, and infusions of CRF specifically into the bed nucleus produced the sustained startle potentiation characteristic of anxiety rather than the phasic potentiation characteristic of conditioned fear.

The clinical import for social anxiety disorder is substantial and has not been adequately absorbed by the SAD imaging literature. The defining phenomenology of SAD is not a phasic conditioned fear response to discrete social cues. It is the sustained, anticipatory apprehension that begins hours or days before a specific evaluative event, the chronic low-grade vigilance during ordinary social interactions before anything has happened, and the contextual quality of the apprehension that spreads across entire social environments rather than attaching to specific stimuli. Patients with SAD are not, in Davis’s framework, primarily organisms showing exaggerated conditioned fear responses at discrete conditioned social stimuli. They are organisms characterized by what the Davis-Walker framework would assign to the bed nucleus: chronically elevated sustained anxiety about uncertain social evaluation that pervades an entire class of contexts. The distinction between a discrete conditioned stimulus driving a phasic central amygdala response and an ambiguous social environment driving sustained bed nucleus apprehension is roughly the distinction between a specific phobia, in which cue-specific conditioned fear is primary, and social anxiety disorder, in which sustained contextual apprehension about social evaluation is the more clinically characteristic feature.

The imaging literature in SAD did not pursue the bed nucleus with intensity proportional to what the phenomenological argument would seem to warrant, and the reasons are partly methodological. The bed nucleus of the stria terminalis is a small structure immediately adjacent to the internal capsule, and at the field strengths and resolutions standard in clinical neuroimaging through the first decade after Birbaumer’s paper, cleanly separating its signal from adjacent amygdaloid structures and white matter required either high-field scanners or specialized acquisition sequences that most anxiety imaging laboratories were not routinely deploying. Somerville, Whalen, and colleagues eventually demonstrated that the human bed nucleus could be measured reliably and that it showed the anticipated pattern of sustained rather than phasic threat responding in healthy individuals. But by the time this work appeared, the amygdala-centered framework for SAD had been institutionally consolidated. Research programs, grant applications, theoretical reviews, and treatment models had all been organized around amygdala hyperreactivity as the central biological feature of the disorder. The bed nucleus became prominent in translational anxiety neuroscience broadly while remaining a secondary stream in SAD specifically.

There is a path-dependence here that is not historically neutral. The Birbaumer paradigm was designed to elicit phasic amygdala responses to face stimuli, and it was designed this way because the Davis circuit model predicted that conditioned social fear would manifest at the central amygdala. That prediction was confirmed; the signal was there. But the paradigm was designed to reveal the central-amygdala-relevant signature of social anxiety, not the bed-nucleus-relevant one. The bed-nucleus-relevant signature requires a different paradigm: not face viewing but sustained anticipatory uncertainty, contextual social threat, the minutes of dread before the speech begins rather than the second a face appears on a screen. If the first imaging study had been designed around that paradigm, and if the acquisition protocol had been built to separate bed nucleus from adjacent amygdala at sufficient resolution, the organizing framework of the subsequent three decades of research might have looked substantially different.

The question that this pair of papers ultimately leaves open is one that the field has approached but has not directly answered: whether the dominant imaging focus on the amygdala in social anxiety reflects the most consequential node in the disorder’s neural circuit, or the most convenient node to find because the circuit model the field inherited was built to point there. Davis’s 1992 blueprint was accurate as an account of conditioned fear circuitry and its central amygdala hub. Birbaumer’s 1998 study confirmed that the relevant structure is hyperreactive in human social phobia and that the hyperreactivity is specific to social stimuli. But five years after the blueprint, Davis’s own work established that a second structure operates in parallel to the one he had originally mapped, mediating the temporal profile of defensive behavior that most closely matches the clinical phenomenology of anxiety disorders. Whether social anxiety is primarily a central amygdala problem, a bed nucleus problem, or some interaction of the two that expresses differently across the anticipatory approach, the in-situation encounter, and the retrospective processing phases of a social event is a question the field now has the technology to answer rigorously: high-field imaging at resolution sufficient to separate the two structures, combined with paradigms designed to tax sustained contextual apprehension rather than phasic face responses. The blueprint Davis drew in 1992 was accurate. What remains genuinely unresolved is whether the first patient Birbaumer scanned in 1998 showed us the most consequential part of it.
