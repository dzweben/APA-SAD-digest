The Social Brain Dispatch

The Arithmetic of Self-Doubt

The brain in social anxiety is not simply overreactive to threat; it is under-responsive to evidence that the threat passed — and the difference between those two failures determines everything about why the disorder persists.

Edition No. 2 · Neural Methods and Computational Accounts · June 25, 2026

Computational psychiatry has been promising precision for a generation. The promise goes like this: if mental disorders can be reformulated as disruptions to specifiable computational operations — inference under uncertainty, prediction error signaling, expected value updating — then diagnosis can escape the unreliability of symptom clusters and treatment can target mechanisms rather than phenomenology. The anxiety disorders have been among the most tractable candidates for this reformulation, because their phenomenology maps so naturally onto the formal vocabulary of learning theory. An anxious person is, by definition, someone whose estimates of the probability and cost of future negative events are systematically too high and whose updating of those estimates in light of disconfirming evidence is systematically too slow. Social anxiety disorder concentrates that problem in a domain uniquely rich in computational complexity: the social evaluation context, where the probability and cost of judgment depend on continuously shifting information about what others see, know, and feel about you.

Two papers across a decade engage this computational program from complementary positions. The first, a theoretical synthesis published in Nature Reviews Neuroscience in 2013, maps the neural circuitry of anxious anticipation through the lens of uncertainty and proposes a five-process model that unifies a decade of empirical neuroscience under a single predictive-computational framework. The second, a 2023 empirical study in Translational Psychiatry, uses reinforcement learning models and fMRI mediation analyses to track what happens, in a social brain under observation, when positive and negative feedback arrive and the process of updating self-evaluation begins. Between them they tell a story about why the anxious brain persists in its fear — one that implicates not only how threat is anticipated but how evidence of safety is encoded or, more precisely, how it fails to be.

Dan Grupe and Jack Nitschke wrote their 2013 review from the Waisman Laboratory for Brain Imaging and Behavior at the University of Wisconsin–Madison. Nitschke built his research program in one of the richest environments in human affective neuroscience: the same institution that housed Richard Davidson’s long arc of work on frontal asymmetry, dispositional emotion, and the biological substrates of well-being and suffering. Davidson’s influence on Wisconsin’s thinking about emotion and its neural correlates is pervasive without being purely inherited. Nitschke’s own empirical trajectory had, for more than a decade before the review, pursued a distinct emphasis on anticipatory neural responding and its relationship to clinical anxiety. His 2006 NeuroImage study identified a distributed circuit including amygdala, anterior insula, anterior cingulate, orbitofrontal cortex, and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex that was recruited both in anticipation of and in response to aversive stimuli — a circuit whose anatomy was telling precisely because it was not centered on the amygdala alone but ran through regions that compute the expected emotional valence of forthcoming events. A 2011 Emotion paper, co-authored with Grupe, had extended this work experimentally: under conditions of uncertain threat, participants showed biased expectancies, overestimating the probability of negative outcomes, and heightened responses to aversive stimuli, with the two effects correlated in a way suggesting the expectancy bias drove rather than merely accompanied the heightened response.

Grupe completed his doctoral work in Nitschke’s lab building this empirical foundation, and the 2013 Nature Reviews Neuroscience paper is where those prior threads converge into a theoretical framework ambitious enough to span translational neuroscience from rodent stress models through human neuroimaging studies of threat anticipation. The paper’s thesis is announced in its opening sentence, borrowed from Daniel Gilbert: the human brain is an “anticipation machine,” and the most consequential thing it does is make the future. The corollary for anxiety is that if the future is uncertain, that machinery runs at cost — and in pathological anxiety, it runs at ruinous cost.

The “uncertainty and anticipation model of anxiety” (UAMA) identifies five processes that function adaptively under threat uncertainty and that go wrong in pathological anxiety. Grupe and Nitschke are careful to note that none of the five is novel in isolation — each has been discussed in prior theoretical accounts — but the advance lies in placing them on equal footing and mapping the specific neural circuitry of each, rather than organizing the field around one primary mechanism. This is both the model’s intellectual honesty and a source of interpretive pressure: a framework with five interacting processes and a distributed set of neural instantiations is rich but not straightforwardly falsifiable by any single study.

The first process is the inflation of threat cost and probability estimates. Anxious individuals across disorders systematically overestimate how likely and how costly negative events will be. For SAD specifically, Foa and colleagues (1996) documented that SAD patients overestimate both the probability and cost of negative social events, and Gilboa-Schechtman and colleagues (2000) showed that their anticipated emotional reactions to social scenarios were more catastrophically negative than the events’ actual emotional impact afterward. Neurally, this reflects disruption of circuits for expected value calculation: the dorsomedial prefrontal cortex and anterior mid-cingulate cortex (aMCC) for probability assessment, and the orbitofrontal cortex for anticipated event cost. The orbitofrontal cortex, which Grupe and Nitschke define as Brodmann areas 11, 13, and 14 plus ventral portions of 10 and 47/12, does not merely respond to primary reinforcers; it represents the integrated value of higher-level outcomes and the expected value of future states, relaying that information to action-selection regions including the striatum and lateral prefrontal cortex. Expected value calculation is not static but dynamically calibrated by reinforcement learning: prediction error signals, the mismatch between expected and actual outcomes generated by midbrain dopaminergic neurons, drive increasingly accurate future predictions for both rewarding and aversive stimuli. Applied to fMRI studies of fear conditioning via temporal difference models (Seymour et al., 2004; Schiller et al., 2008; Li et al., 2011), aversive prediction errors are localized to the ventral striatum, anterior insula, and rostral cingulate cortex. Grupe and Nitschke propose that disrupted negative prediction error signaling in anxiety disorders — specifically, blunted signaling when the feared outcome fails to materialize — prevents the appropriate downward revision of threat estimates and maintains inflated expectancies in the face of accumulating disconfirming evidence.

The second process is heightened threat attention and hypervigilance. The amygdala’s tonic hyperactivity in anxiety disorders is well documented — Etkin and Wager’s 2007 meta-analysis established bilateral amygdala hyperactivation as the most consistent shared finding across PTSD, SAD, and specific phobia. Grupe and Nitschke situate this within a more mechanistically specific account. The Pearce–Hall learning model assigns the central nucleus of the amygdala (CeA) the role of monitoring stimulus associability: directing attentional resources toward stimuli that have been paired with surprising outcomes and away from those reliably followed by benign events. When the CeA is tonically elevated, as in anxious individuals, its signal-to-noise ratio is degraded. It can no longer efficiently identify which cues most demand attentional processing and instead broadcasts broadly toward threat, producing the hypervigilance that makes social situations feel saturated with judgment. In SAD, the evidence is specifically anticipatory: heightened resting amygdala blood flow has been documented (Furmark et al., 2002), amygdala hyperactivity during the anticipation period before public speech delivery has been measured (Lorberbaum et al., 2004), and Guyer and colleagues (2008) found amygdala and ventrolateral PFC activation in children with SAD anticipating unknown peer feedback, suggesting that the bias is present before clinical chronicity has fully established itself.

The third process is deficient safety learning. Environmental safety signals — cues that reliably predict the absence of harm — relieve anticipatory anxiety by resolving uncertainty downward. The neural basis of their acquisition involves a vmPFC–amygdala inhibitory circuit homologous to the rodent infralimbic cortex–amygdala axis, where infralimbic stimulation suppresses conditioned fear expression and inactivation impairs extinction recall. In SAD the structural evidence is particularly striking: Phan and colleagues (2009) documented microstructural abnormalities of the uncinate fasciculus — the primary white matter pathway connecting ventral prefrontal cortex with medial temporal lobe structures including the amygdala — in patients with generalized SAD compared to controls, suggesting that the anatomical substrate for effective vmPFC–amygdala inhibitory communication is itself compromised. The behavioral complement is the interpretation bias literature: Stopa and Clark (2000) showed that SAD patients interpret ambiguous social scenarios as threatening at rates exceeding controls, and Yoon and Zinbarg (2008) demonstrated that interpreting neutral faces as threatening is effectively a default mode for socially anxious individuals — findings consistent with a system that has not adequately learned that social neutrality signals safety.

The fourth process is behavioral and cognitive avoidance. Avoidance prevents the accumulation of disconfirmatory evidence. When a socially anxious person exits the feared situation early, or occupies themselves with worry in a way that keeps emotional engagement at arm’s length, they never encounter the sustained disconfirming experience that would revise the inflated threat estimate. The neural circuitry involves the amygdala’s interaction with OFC, striatum, lateral PFC, aMCC, and anterior insula in action selection under uncertainty, with the anterior insula playing a specific role: it generates anticipatory emotional responses to hypothetical future states, answering the question “how is it going to feel?” That question, answered negatively under uncertain social conditions, drives avoidance as much as the fear itself does. Grupe and Nitschke note that when spider phobics were successfully treated, activity in the anterior insula and aMCC decreased and dorsolateral PFC activity increased — a pattern consistent with the cost of tolerating uncertainty declining and regulatory efficiency rising.

The fifth process is heightened reactivity to threat uncertainty itself, and this is where the most important neurobiological distinction in the model lives: the CeA versus the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis (BNST). The CeA coordinates phasic, cue-specific defensive responses to immediate and unambiguous threats. The BNST mediates sustained, diffuse responding under conditions of unpredictable threat — conditions in which the source of danger is not localized and the timing is unclear. The distinction is pharmacologically specific: benzodiazepines reduce fear-potentiated startle to unpredictable but not predictable threats, in both rodents and humans, because they act at least partly through the BNST. Clinically, Grillon and colleagues (2008, 2009) showed that panic disorder and PTSD patients have elevated startle specifically during temporal unpredictability conditions while being indistinguishable from controls when threat is predictable, directly implicating BNST-mediated uncertainty responding rather than general amygdala excitability as the pathological mechanism. Spider phobics showed greater BNST activity than controls during temporally unpredictable anticipation of spider images (Straube et al., 2007), and BNST activation correlated with trait anxiety under uncertain threat conditions in healthy individuals (Somerville et al., 2010). The anterior insula, continuously feeding somatic predictions about forthcoming aversive events into this circuit, contributes to BNST hyperactivity through its connections with both BNST and amygdala. Intolerance of uncertainty — the dispositional inability to accept the possibility of negative events regardless of their probability — is elevated in SAD (Carleton et al., 2012) and correlates with anterior and mid-insula responses to affectively ambiguous faces (Simmons et al., 2008), consistent with the anterior insula as the neural substrate of uncertainty’s felt unbearability.

The aMCC stands over all five processes as an integrative node for adaptive control. Adopting the framework of Shackman and colleagues’ 2011 Nature Reviews Neuroscience review, Grupe and Nitschke position the aMCC as the structure that receives convergent input from negative affect, pain, cognitive control, and interoceptive systems and translates that convergence into appropriate action selection and regulatory response. Its structural connectivity with anterior insula, amygdala, dorsolateral PFC, parietal cortex, and motor regions gives it the anatomical authority to serve this role. When the aMCC is impaired or its connections are disrupted, the five UAMA processes compound without effective executive arbitration: inflated threat estimates feed hypervigilance, hypervigilance prevents safety learning, avoidance prevents safety learning independently, and the resulting sustained uncertainty further amplifies BNST-mediated reactivity in a loop that nothing is positioned to break. In SAD, reduced aMCC–anterior insula functional connectivity during the viewing of fearful faces (Klumpp et al., 2012) represents precisely this hub disruption, while increased aMCC activation in anxious adolescents during uncertainty-laden decision-making (Krain et al., 2008) suggests the region may be effortfully recruited but failing to achieve the integrative clarity it provides in non-anxious individuals.

The UAMA is a theoretical scaffold, built on a decade of neuroimaging and translational research, and it was largely composed before the computational tools needed to test its RL predictions cleanly were applied to social anxiety disorder specifically. Grupe and Nitschke could point to disrupted prediction error signaling as a proposed mechanism and cite temporal difference models applied to general fear conditioning paradigms, but the study that would directly measure asymmetric learning rate parameters in SAD patients using a genuinely social stimulus, and then tie those parameters to identified brain mediators via formal causal analysis, did not yet exist in 2013. It arrived a decade later from a team assembled across two continents to do exactly that.

Leonie Koban, who holds a position at the Lyon Neuroscience Research Center in France, works on the computational neuroscience of social cognition and self-referential processing under social evaluation. Her collaboration with Jessica Andrews-Hanna brought in a perspective the UAMA does not supply: the default mode network’s contribution to self-model construction and social self-evaluation. Andrews-Hanna’s laboratory at the University of Arizona has been among the leading forces in establishing that the medial prefrontal and posterior cingulate hubs of the DMN actively support narrative self-representation, mental simulation of social scenarios, and the ongoing evaluation of self-concept in social context — not merely default activity in the absence of task demands. For a disorder defined in large part by a chronic negative narrative about the self as it appears to others, the DMN’s computational role in self-referential processing is directly clinically relevant in ways that the classic threat-detection circuit literature does not reach.

Tor Wager, at Dartmouth’s Department of Cognitive and Brain Sciences, is among the most methodologically consequential figures in clinical neuroimaging. His laboratory built the neurological pain signature — a multivariate fMRI pattern that reliably tracks pain intensity across individuals with classification accuracy exceeding any single brain region — and has developed the model-based fMRI mediation framework that Koban and colleagues deployed here: a method that fits behavioral data to a computational model, extracts trial-by-trial model-derived regressors, and asks which brain regions carry the path between model-computed quantities and behavioral outcomes. Joanna Arch at CU Boulder brings the clinical science anchor: her work on acceptance-based CBT and its neural mechanisms grounds the team in the translational question of whether learning rate asymmetries can be shifted by treatment and what the neural signature of that shift looks like.

The experimental paradigm was a social evaluation context built around public speaking — the single situation most consistently rated as maximally anxiety-provoking in SAD samples. Participants gave a speech about their ideal job to a panel, across multiple trials, and received positive or negative feedback on their performance. Before and after each round of feedback, they rated their self-evaluation and state self-esteem on standardized scales. The reinforcement learning model fit each participant’s sequence of ratings and feedback to recover two parameters: a positive feedback learning rate (α+) quantifying how much the participant updated self-evaluation upward in response to favorable social feedback, and a negative feedback learning rate (α−) quantifying how much they updated downward in response to critical feedback. The ratio of these rates is the computational signature of the evaluation bias.

Twenty-one participants meeting DSM-5 criteria for SAD and twenty-three matched healthy controls completed the protocol. The behavioral results were unambiguous. SAD participants showed significantly greater updating of self-evaluation and state self-esteem in response to negative compared to positive social feedback — α− exceeded α+ substantially. Controls showed the opposite: a positivity bias allocating more self-evaluation updating to favorable feedback than to criticism, the default that protects self-concept under ordinary social evaluation. The asymmetry between groups was not a matter of SAD patients simply being more reactive to all social feedback; it was specifically about the direction. Good news moved SAD self-evaluations less. Bad news moved them more. In RL terms, the positive prediction error signal — the social equivalent of “this went better than I expected” — was being systematically discounted.

The fMRI mediation analyses asked which brain regions carried the path from feedback mismatch — the discrepancy between what was expected and what the panel actually delivered — to the resulting change in self-evaluation. Two structures played distinct roles, and a third supplied the most theoretically consequential finding.

The anterior insula and frontal operculum mediated the negative feedback bias in SAD. When negative feedback arrived, activity in this region tracked both the magnitude of the prediction error and the degree of subsequent self-evaluation updating, and the strength of this mediation was greater in SAD than in controls. The connection to the UAMA is direct: Grupe and Nitschke placed anterior insula dysfunction at the center of Process 5, as the region that generates somatic predictions about forthcoming aversive events and that shows heightened activity under conditions of threat uncertainty. Koban and colleagues locate it now not only in anticipation but in the encoding of feedback already received — the signal has arrived, and the anterior insula is amplifying its impact on the self-model. The same structure predicted to drive hyperreactive anticipatory anxiety also mediates hyperreactive learning from negative social outcomes. It is doing what the UAMA said it does, but at a different moment in the anxiety cycle than the UAMA emphasized.

The vmPFC played the opposite role: buffering the social influence effect, such that greater vmPFC activity was associated with less self-evaluation updating from feedback regardless of valence. This implicates the vmPFC in a broader regulatory function extending beyond the safety-learning and extinction-recall account Grupe and Nitschke give it under Process 3. In the Koban et al. paradigm, the vmPFC is moderating in real time how much social information from outside is allowed to penetrate and revise the self-concept. It is doing something like what a well-regulated person does naturally: maintaining a stable internal model of self against the press of external social evaluation, letting feedback inform but not destabilize. The finding provides a computational mechanism for the therapeutic observation that improving emotion regulation improves social function in SAD — not because regulation dampens the amygdala’s fear response but because it stabilizes the self-model against evaluative volatility.

The most theoretically significant finding involves the frontoparietal network. FPN activity showed reduced responses to positive feedback in SAD compared to controls: the network that coordinates top-down attentional control and working memory updating was less engaged precisely when favorable social information arrived. Koban and colleagues interpret this as a failure of regulatory amplification for positive content. The FPN normally helps elaborate, hold, and integrate positive social feedback into the running self-representation; in SAD this elaboration fails. Good news does not receive the top-down attentional prioritization needed to enter and revise the self-model. The FPN barely figures in the UAMA’s circuit diagrams, which are organized around limbic and paralimbic structures. Its appearance here reflects Andrews-Hanna’s contribution: the recognition that social self-referential processing involves not only circuits for threat detection but cortical networks for narrative self-modeling, and that the failure in SAD may live at least as much in the latter as in the former.

The treatment implication Koban and colleagues identify is one of the most clinically pointed findings in recent SAD neuroscience: CBT normalizes FPN activity in SAD. If that finding replicates with adequate power, it suggests that successful psychological treatment is not primarily working by dampening amygdala reactivity or strengthening vmPFC extinction output, though it may do some of both, but also by restoring the frontoparietal capacity to process and integrate positive social information. The mechanism of change in CBT may include the re-education of the positive learning rate.

Read against each other, these papers converge in important places and create genuine friction in others.

Both implicate the anterior insula as a central locus of the problem. Grupe and Nitschke place it at the interface of uncertainty, interoception, and anticipated emotional cost; Koban and colleagues find it mediating the encoding of actual negative social feedback. The implication is that the same structure performs double computational work: it generates biased predictions about how bad the upcoming encounter will be, and it amplifies the impact of negative social information when that information actually arrives. The insula is both the loudspeaker of anticipated threat and the amplifier of received criticism. The UAMA predicted the first function; the Koban study finds both.

Both implicate the vmPFC, though they assign it somewhat different roles. Grupe and Nitschke emphasize its inhibitory function in extinction and safety learning; Koban and colleagues find it moderating the social influence loop on self-evaluation. These are consistent rather than contradictory: a region that both signals safety and buffers external evaluative pressure is performing a unified job of maintaining the internal regulatory environment against threat, whether that threat is the failure of safety signals to reduce anticipatory alarm or the pressure of another person’s negative opinion arriving and demanding self-model revision.

The friction lies in what each account emphasizes and what it cannot see. The UAMA is an anticipatory model. Its thesis is that anxiety is fundamentally future-oriented, and its neural signature is most visible in the moments before a social encounter arrives — the BNST firing before the speech begins, the amygdala hypervigilant in the approach corridor, the anterior insula generating somatic dread in advance of the panel’s verdict. That is a beautifully specified claim about a specific moment in the anxiety cycle. Koban and colleagues study the aftermath: how feedback is encoded after the encounter and used to update the self-model that shapes what is anticipated next time. These are not competing claims about the same moment; they are claims about different moments. But their clinical implications diverge in a way that matters.

If the primary problem is anticipatory — BNST-driven sustained alarm, amygdala hypervigilance during social approach, vmPFC-amygdala connectivity disruption preventing safety consolidation — then the most powerful therapeutic target is disrupting the anticipatory loop. Exposure to feared situations before anxiety drives avoidance allows the BNST circuit to habituate and the vmPFC to consolidate safety memories across successful social encounters. This is the logic of every evidence-based exposure treatment for SAD. If the primary problem is learning-based — a positive learning rate selectively blunted, a FPN that fails to elaborate favorable evidence into the self-model — then exposure alone may not deliver. The patient who survives the social encounter, receives mild positive feedback, and processes that feedback through a system that systematically downweights positive social prediction errors will not update threat estimates toward the benign end regardless of how many exposures accumulate. The disconfirming evidence is there; the mechanism for absorbing it is biased.

The deeper unresolved question is whether these are one failure or two. The UAMA proposes disrupted aversive prediction error signaling — specifically, blunted signaling when the feared outcome fails to occur — as the mechanism by which anxious individuals maintain inflated threat expectancies despite accumulating evidence to the contrary. Koban and colleagues document the complementary side: when positive social feedback arrives, α+ is selectively depressed. These might be the same disruption encountered in different paradigms: a system that cannot update threat estimates toward safety from either direction — neither from the non-occurrence of harm nor from the occurrence of welcome social validation. Or they might be partially dissociable deficits: the BNST-mediated anticipatory response and the FPN-mediated learning response could reflect somewhat independent computational failures that together produce the full clinical picture. If they dissociate across individuals, some patients predominantly characterized by hyperreactive anticipation and others by blunted positive learning, the implication for treatment matching is immediate. What appears to be a homogeneous disorder defined by the symptom of social fear might decompose, at the computational level, into partially distinct subtypes with partially distinct optimal treatments.

What neither paper can yet answer is the temporal direction of the relationship: does the BNST-mediated anticipatory alarm shape how FPN processes subsequent feedback — the alarm state biasing what information gets amplified in the aftermath — or do they operate in relative independence, driven by partially separable neural substrates that converge on the same clinical presentation? Longitudinal studies that fit RL models to both the anticipatory and learning phases of social evaluation trials, in the same patients before and after treatment, combining resting-state and task-based fMRI with formal causal mediation, are technically achievable. They have not been done. The arithmetic of self-doubt needs to be measured in real time, across the full social encounter cycle, if we are going to understand not just what goes wrong but in what order.
