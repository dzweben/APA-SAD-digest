The Social Brain Dispatch

Learning the Wrong Lesson

The social learning system runs on two neuromodulatory channels with distinct neural substrates; social anxiety disorder disrupts one of them, and the experiment capable of saying which has not yet been done.

Edition No. 2 · Neural Methods and Computational Accounts · June 18, 2026

The dominant explanatory vocabulary for social anxiety disorder has always been the vocabulary of alarm. The amygdala fires too readily, arousal overtakes cognition, the social world becomes a theater of anticipated condemnation. That vocabulary maps onto clinical phenomenology well enough: the racing heart, the sweating, the certainty of being seen and found wanting. But it is a reactive vocabulary, designed to explain the organism caught in the grip of a stimulus. It does not easily explain what happens between social encounters, in the accumulating record of social experience that the brain keeps across a lifetime and that a person with SAD consults continuously: a record systematically skewed negative in ways that simple threat-reactivity accounts cannot reach, and that persists long after any single threatening encounter has ended.

The more tractable question is not how the SAD brain responds to a threatening face but how it learns from what other people show it about itself. Framed that way, the problem becomes computational. It concerns the parameters of a learning system: how much weight the system assigns to positive versus negative social feedback, how stable its prior beliefs are in the face of incoming information, how precisely it tracks the reliability of the social source from which that information comes. These quantities are not metaphors. They can be estimated from behavioral data, grounded in specific neural mechanisms, and their individual variation predicts clinical outcomes in ways that raw neuroimaging measures alone generally do not.

Two papers, from very different laboratories and using very different approaches, have now made this framing unavoidable. The first, published in Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience in 2017 by Andreea Diaconescu, Christoph Mathys, and colleagues at the Translational Neuromodeling Unit in Zurich, established the neural architecture of healthy hierarchical social learning using one of the most mathematically rigorous models in computational psychiatry. The second, published in Translational Psychiatry in 2023 by Leonie Koban, Tor Wager, and colleagues, applied a related but simpler computational framework to social feedback learning specifically in SAD, identifying the neural mediators of the negative learning bias that defines the disorder at the behavioral level. Read together, they produce a picture whose full implications neither achieves on its own.

Klaas Stephan’s Translational Neuromodeling Unit, operating jointly at ETH Zurich and the University of Zurich, is one of the genuinely unusual scientific environments that the computational psychiatry movement has produced. Stephan trained under Karl Friston at the Wellcome Trust Centre for Neuroimaging in London, inheriting both a mathematical framework and an institutional ambition: the conviction that formal generative models are clinical instruments rather than explanatory metaphors, and that the rigorous specification of individual differences in computational parameters is the path from population-level diagnostic categories to mechanistic accounts of individual patients. The TNU brought that ambition to Zurich with the explicit program of developing translational tools, specifically the mathematical models and statistical infrastructure, that could move from research laboratory to clinical practice. Friston’s free-energy framework and his formalization of the brain as a hierarchical inference engine provided the intellectual foundation; what the TNU added was the clinical and statistical apparatus for applying that framework to heterogeneous patient populations and for doing so in ways that could eventually inform individual-level clinical decisions.

Christoph Mathys had developed the Hierarchical Gaussian Filter within this context, and its presence in the 2017 paper as the central computational tool was the direct contribution of the model’s architect. The HGF is a Bayesian filter for hierarchical inference built around a single core insight: the world has multiple levels of structure, and a rational brain must track all of them simultaneously. There is what is happening right now (the specific outcome of this interaction), there is the tendency of the environment to produce particular kinds of outcomes (the base rate), and there is how fast that tendency itself is changing (volatility). A system tracking only the first level is maximally sensitive but maximally confused by noise. A system tracking all three can allocate the precision of its belief updates appropriately, weighting each observation by how informative it is given the estimated volatility of the underlying state. When volatility is high, individual observations should carry less weight because any single event is less informative about the stable underlying state. When volatility is low, they should carry more. The HGF formalizes that precision-weighting as a set of coupled differential equations with parameters that are estimable from behavioral data and testable against neural signals via model-based fMRI, in which the trial-by-trial prediction error timeseries generated by the model is used as a parametric regressor rather than a condition-average contrast.

Andreea Diaconescu, as lead and corresponding author, brought the HGF to a social learning paradigm that is structurally simple and inferentially demanding. Eighty-two healthy male adults, divided into two independent fMRI samples of 35 and 47 participants, made predictions about which of two colored options would win a binary lottery while receiving advice from a videotaped confederate adviser whose hidden incentive structure changed covertly across 189 trials per session. During cooperative phases the adviser was motivated to help the participant win; during adversarial phases, to mislead. Participants received no direct signal about the adviser’s current motivation and had to infer it from the trial-by-trial pattern of advice accuracy, updating that inference continuously as the adviser’s motivation shifted. The design forced inference at exactly the two levels the HGF is built to separate: the low-level question of whether this particular piece of advice was accurate, and the high-level question of how fast the adviser’s reliability is changing. Both samples performed well above chance (Study 1: 68%; Study 2: 67%), confirming that the social learning signal was behaviorally effective.

The three-level HGF fit to each participant’s choices yielded four free parameters. Kappa (κ) captures the coupling strength between the second and third levels of the hierarchy, how much the running estimate of adviser volatility is allowed to influence trial-by-trial trust updates. Omega (ω) captures baseline log-volatility independent of the volatility estimate, a subject-specific tendency to update beliefs rapidly or slowly. Theta (ϑ) captures the evolution rate of meta-volatility itself. Zeta (ζ) weights the contribution of social advice against the participant’s own direct perceptual information about the lottery probabilities. Formal model comparison using protected exceedance probabilities confirmed the three-level HGF over all simpler alternatives in both samples (exceedance probabilities 0.92 and 0.94 for the full model; family-level inference further confirmed that the hierarchical structure, with volatility tracking, outperformed all non-hierarchical models at exceedance probabilities of 0.94 and 1.0). Parameters jointly accounted for 28–39% of performance variance across samples; social weighting (ζ) was the strongest single predictor (F = 7.08–7.72, p < 0.01 in both samples), explaining 15–18% of variance individually. The degree to which a participant weighted social information relative to direct perceptual evidence turned out to be the most consequential individual-difference parameter for how well they navigated a social environment where that information was partly unreliable.

The fMRI analysis entered the HGF-derived prediction error timeseries as parametric regressors in a standard GLM with physiological noise correction and six motion regressors, and sought the neural substrates of the two prediction error levels by asking which brain regions tracked each timeseries across trials. Low-level prediction errors, the signed discrepancy between actual advice outcome and the brain’s expected outcome given its current trust estimate, activated a network confirmed by conjunction analysis across both independent samples. The ventral tegmental area and substantia nigra appeared at MNI coordinates [9, −15, −9] (t = 3.81); right anterior insula at [36, 20, −2] (t = 6.00); left anterior insula at [−38, 18, −5] (t = 4.77); anterior cingulate cortex at [8, 32, 27] (t = 4.24). In the second sample, bilateral temporoparietal junction was additionally confirmed at peak t-values reaching 8.93, plausibly reflecting the mentalizing demands of updating beliefs about a social agent’s momentary intentions. High-level prediction errors, precision-weighted updates about the volatility of adviser fidelity that fire not when a single piece of advice is wrong but when the pattern of accuracy is changing faster or slower than anticipated, activated a categorically different substrate: the cholinergic septum, confirmed in both samples at left [−5, 12, −7] (t = 2.9), alongside dorsal anterior cingulate cortex. These two activations were confirmed by the logical AND conjunction across FWE-corrected maps from both independent samples, a conservative replication criterion that controlled for the false-positive risk that any single study carries.

A genetic analysis sharpened the neuromodulatory interpretation. COMT Met/Met carriers, who maintain relatively higher tonic dopamine availability due to reduced enzymatic catabolism, showed significantly enhanced striatal activation in response to low-level social prediction errors across both samples (left striatum in Study 1 and Study 2 under peak and cluster-level FWE correction respectively), but showed no genotype effects on high-level prediction error responses. The COMT polymorphism selectively modulated the dopaminergic channel and left the cholinergic one untouched. The dissociation is not a regional curiosity. It is a mechanistic claim: moment-to-moment surprise at social information is dopaminergic, and the higher-order uncertainty about how fast the social world is changing is cholinergic. Both are part of the architecture of healthy social learning. Either, in principle, could be disrupted by disease, by pharmacology, or by experience.

The Diaconescu study concerns inference about an external social agent in an outcome domain that has nothing to do with the self. The lottery game is about predicting colored options; the advice is instrumental; the adviser’s reliability affects whether the participant wins money, not what kind of person they are. When social feedback shifts from the instrumental domain to the self-referential one, when the message is not “here is what I predict will happen” but “here is what I think of you,” the computational and neural landscape changes in ways the Diaconescu paradigm cannot access. Koban, Andrews-Hanna, Ives, Wager, and Arch moved the question precisely there.

Leonie Koban’s laboratory at the Lyon Neuroscience Research Center investigates how social environments shape affective states and self-representation. The collaboration with Tor Wager’s Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience Lab at Dartmouth brought to this clinical question the mediation-based fMRI framework that Wager’s group has developed as a method for formally testing whether a specific brain region carries the signal connecting a causal input to a behavioral output, rather than merely co-activating with both. Wager’s laboratory is among the few neuroimaging groups that has consistently demanded this level of causal specificity rather than settling for activated-regions-associated-with-conditions, and that demand shaped the entire analytic architecture of the Koban study. Jessica Andrews-Hanna, who established much of what the field knows about the default mode network and its role in self-referential mental activity, contributed both conceptual framing and analytic expertise on the specific systems through which a self-model is updated in response to social input. Joanna Arch, a clinical psychologist at the University of Colorado Boulder with an active SAD treatment research program, grounded the clinical design in diagnostic rigor: all participants were assessed with the Anxiety and Related Disorders Interview Schedule for DSM-5, comorbidities were screened with the Mini International Neuropsychiatric Interview, and control participants were excluded for any current anxiety disorder rather than merely for high trait anxiety scores on a questionnaire.

The forty-four participants (21 meeting full DSM-5 criteria for generalized SAD, 23 matched healthy controls) first prepared and delivered a three-minute speech about their ideal job to two confederate judges in the scanner environment. The subsequent feedback phase, with 32 participants completing it inside the scanner on a Siemens TrioTim or Prisma 3T with T2*-weighted EPI at TR = 1.3s, presented 52 trials in which an evaluative cue related to an aspect of the speech appeared, the participant rated their self-evaluation on a visual analogue scale, judges’ feedback appeared as a cursor on that same scale, and the participant then rated state self-esteem. Feedback was generated from a distribution centered on each participant’s own self-evaluation, producing approximately equal proportions of positive and negative prediction errors within each session. Any asymmetry in how the brain responded to the two types of mismatch was therefore attributable to the system’s internal weighting of positive versus negative social information, not to any difference in the objective frequency or magnitude of the feedback signal across groups.

The computational model was a Rescorla-Wagner variant with separate learning rates for positive and negative affective prediction errors. State self-esteem at each trial updated as: FeelingSelf(t) = FeelingSelf(t−1) + α_SelfPos × APE when feedback was better than expected, and FeelingSelf(t) = FeelingSelf(t−1) + α_SelfNeg × APE when feedback was worse than expected. Bayesian model comparison confirmed the two-parameter model over the single-rate alternative. The asymmetry between the two rates is the computational signature the study turns on. A system with α_SelfPos > α_SelfNeg assigns more weight to confirming evidence of social regard, maintaining the self-enhancement bias documented across the social psychological literature on healthy adults and predicted by clinical theory to be absent or reversed in SAD.

Both predictions were confirmed. The group-by-valence interaction on model-derived learning rates reached t(25.0) = 2.75, p = 0.011, Cohen’s d = 0.84. Healthy controls showed a robust positive bias (α_SelfPos > α_SelfNeg: t(22) = 3.91, p < 0.001, d = 0.82). SAD patients showed neither the positive bias nor a consistently negative one, with the valence asymmetry trending negative but not significant (t(20) = −1.52, p = 0.143, d = 0.33). The affective updating bias correlated with a separately computed social influence bias on explicit self-evaluation changes (r = 0.54, p < 0.001), and both correlated with anxiety and depression scores across the full sample. SAD patients also entered the task with significantly lower initial state self-esteem than controls (t(35.0) = 2.40, p = 0.022, d = 0.73), a result pointing simultaneously to a distorted prior and distorted learning dynamics. These are separable computational phenomena, even though the single-level framework used here cannot fully disentangle them.

The neural results connected the learning-rate asymmetry to a specific pattern of network dysfunction. Frontoparietal network activation to social feedback was reduced overall in SAD patients relative to controls (t(29.9) = 2.23, p = 0.034, d = 0.79), and FPN valence modulation, the degree to which the network differentially amplified positive relative to negative feedback signals, was also attenuated (t(29.9) = 2.11, p = 0.043, d = 0.75). Individual variation in FPN valence modulation correlated with the positive learning bias across participants (Spearman’s r = 0.35–0.46 across related measures), suggesting that the FPN’s differential sensitivity to positive social information is part of the mechanism by which healthy brains maintain the self-enhancement tilt, and that its attenuation in SAD is the neural signature of that tilt’s absence. The mediation analysis identified the anterior insula and frontal operculum as the pathway through which negative social feedback suppresses self-evaluation. The three-path mediation confirmed right insula and adjacent frontal operculum as the principal node for Path a (negative feedback mismatch to AI/FO activation), Path b (AI/FO activation to depressed self-evaluation at follow-up), and Path ab (the indirect mediated effect), confirmed in the conjunction analysis across all three paths. The vmPFC appeared as a partial suppressor, exerting an opposing negative mediation effect that partially counteracted the insula’s downward pull on self-evaluation, a result that hints at an intact but insufficient buffering mechanism in SAD.

The convergence between these papers is anatomically specific and theoretically productive. In Diaconescu et al. (2017), anterior insula was among the most robustly confirmed regions for low-level social prediction errors in healthy adults, bilaterally, at peak t-values of 4.77 and 6.00 in the conjunction across two independent samples. In Koban et al. (2023), anterior insula is the principal mediating structure through which negative social feedback reaches and lowers self-evaluation. In both cases, the insula is transducing a social discrepancy signal, the gap between expected and received social information, into a motivationally and self-referentially weighted update. That function is consistent with the insula’s established role in interoceptive inference and in integrating prediction errors across affective and bodily state dimensions. Both papers arrive at the same structure as the site where social information is converted into a signal that changes the model of the self, and they arrive there from opposite starting points: one from the computational architecture of healthy social learning, the other from the clinical phenomenology of social anxiety’s learning failure.

The tension between the papers is equally specific and equally consequential. Diaconescu et al. established that healthy social learning runs on two distinct neuromodulatory channels: dopaminergic midbrain for the moment-to-moment surprise at social outcomes, and cholinergic septum for the higher-order estimate of how volatile and therefore unpredictable the social environment is. Koban et al. operate with a single-level model that tracks how each feedback event updates self-esteem but does not distinguish whether the learning-rate asymmetry they observe reflects a disrupted low-level prediction error signal, a disrupted high-level volatility estimate, or some combination of both. The behavioral signatures of these two failures are not separable with a Rescorla-Wagner framework. A SAD patient with a blunted dopaminergic response to positive social prediction errors will show a reduced α_SelfPos because positive surprises fail to register as strongly in the self-update computation. But a SAD patient with an elevated cholinergic estimate of social volatility will also show a pattern that weights negative feedback more heavily: high volatility estimates cause the brain to revise its beliefs more drastically in response to any discrepant signal, and when the prior self-model is negative, the largest discrepancies tend to be negative events, so the volatility-driven amplification asymmetrically benefits negative feedback. These are mechanistically different stories that predict different responses to pharmacological interventions and different targets for brain stimulation or neuromodulatory approaches.

Diaconescu et al. are not free of limitation from the other direction. Their paradigm concerns inference about an external social agent’s reliability in an instrumental domain. When feedback touches the self-concept, the neural systems engaged by self-referential processing, including medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate, and angular gyrus, come online in ways the adviser-trustworthiness task does not engage. The theory-of-mind network the Diaconescu paradigm reliably recruits, specifically temporoparietal junction and superior temporal sulcus, is not coextensive with the default mode network activated during self-referential thought. The boundary between these systems matters for a disorder whose core pathology is in the self-relevant domain. A parameter like κ, coupling the volatility estimate to moment-to-moment trust updates, may mean something categorically different when what is being tracked is one’s standing in the eyes of others versus the reliability of an informant about lottery colors. The HGF parameters estimated from an adviser-trustworthiness task may not transfer cleanly to a self-evaluation context without additional validation in a paradigm that spans both domains.

There is also something in the Koban finding of distorted prior self-esteem that deserves attention beyond what the study’s model can provide. The significantly lower initial state self-esteem in SAD patients (d = 0.73) precedes any within-session feedback. It is a structural property of the self-model that the learning phase is operating on, not a product of that phase. Whether this prior distortion is computationally independent of the learning-rate asymmetry, or whether the two maintain each other in a self-reinforcing loop across development, in which a negative prior generates predictions of negative evaluation that make positive feedback seem more surprising and therefore less credible while making negative feedback seem merely confirmatory, is a question the cross-sectional single-session design cannot resolve. The HGF has formal machinery for separating prior-level distortions from likelihood-level distortions, but only when the paradigm explicitly manipulates the informativeness and reliability of the feedback signal across conditions, as the Diaconescu design does and the Koban design does not.

The unresolved question at the intersection of these two papers is the most actionable one the field currently faces in the computational study of social anxiety. At which level of the Diaconescu hierarchy does the fault lie? If the disruption is primarily at the prior level, a self-model so negative that incoming positive evidence cannot update it, then interventions targeting the learning signal will accomplish little because the error is in what the signal is being compared against rather than in the signal itself. If the disruption is at the first level, in the dopaminergic transduction of positive social prediction errors into self-model updates, then the point of failure is at the moment of feedback reception and could in principle be shifted by pharmacological or brain stimulation approaches that modulate dopaminergic signaling during social evaluation contexts. If the disruption is at the second level, in the cholinergic estimate of social volatility, then the brain has concluded that the social world is too unpredictable to trust, and the appropriate intervention is not to adjust any individual feedback signal but to recalibrate the epistemic state governing how much updating should occur at all. These are different experiments, different pharmacological probes, different targets for transcranial magnetic stimulation or transcranial direct current stimulation applied to the cholinergic basal forebrain.

The experiment that would close most of these gaps is specifiable with current technology. It would apply a full hierarchical computational model, an HGF-class filter with at least three levels, to a social self-evaluation feedback paradigm in which participants receive feedback about themselves from sources whose reliability varies covertly across sessions, enabling separate estimation of first-level learning precision and second-level volatility tracking in the self-referential domain. Applied in a clinical sample with confirmed SAD and matched controls, it would distinguish the three computationally distinct contributors to the negative learning bias: a distorted prior (the self-model starts from a negative baseline), a distorted first-level learning rate (positive social prediction errors fail to update the self-model as strongly as negative ones), and a distorted second-level volatility estimate (the brain treats the social world as more unpredictable than it is, amplifying all prediction errors and asymmetrically benefiting negative feedback when the prior is negative). The HGF has the mathematical apparatus to adjudicate among these possibilities. The Koban paradigm has the clinical and ecological validity to make the findings relevant to actual SAD phenomenology. What does not yet exist is a study that brings both frameworks to the same clinical sample and the same question simultaneously. When that study arrives, it will not merely describe what is broken in social anxiety disorder. It will say, with mechanistic precision, where the break is.
